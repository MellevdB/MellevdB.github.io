Assessing Image Quality in Photoacoustic Imaging: A Metric-Based and Learning-Based Evaluation

This thesis explores the automatic and reliable assessment of image quality in photoacoustic imaging (PAI), a hybrid modality that combines light and ultrasound to produce high-contrast images. Altough it is getting more important in biomedical applications, the evaluation of PAI image quality remains challenging due to artefacts, noise, and the lack of standardized assessment methods. To address this, we systematically evaluated a wide range of full-reference (FR) and no-reference (NR) image quality metrics on diverse experimental and simulated PAI datasets, including clinical and phantom images. Metric scores were analyzed statistically to determine which best distinguish reconstruction quality across varying image configurations. We then trained and evaluated three deep learning models, PhotoacousticQualityNet, IQDCNN, and EfficientNetIQA, to predict these metric scores directly from reconstructed images, enabling automatic image quality assessment without a reference. The results show that structural FR metrics such as SSIM, S3IM, and IWSSIM are the most reliable in capturing quality differences in PAI data, while PSNR and NR metrics like BRISQUE and CLIP-IQA underperformed. Deep learning models achieved accurate score predictions, particularly on in-distribution data, with EfficientNetIQA showing the best overall performance. However, interpretability analysis revealed that the models did not always focus on clinically relevant regions. When compared with expert preferences collected in a survey, models trained on structure-aware metrics aligned best with human judgment. This study provides a benchmark for image quality assessment in photoacoustic imaging and demonstrates the potential of deep learning to automate this process. The findings suggest that structure-sensitive metrics and explainable models are essential for improving PAI image evaluation and integrating quality control into clinical workflows.
